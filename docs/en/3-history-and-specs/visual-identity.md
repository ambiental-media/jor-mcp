# From information to flow: the process of creating the JOR-MCP visual identity

The development of the JOR-MCP visual identity stemmed from a central challenge: to visually translate the operation of a tool based on processing, organizing, and returning information. The goal was to build a visual system capable of communicating technology, clarity, and reliability.

## Research and Direction Definition
The first stage involved searching for visual references related to technology, artificial intelligence, data flows, digital interfaces, and information systems. In parallel, an analysis of Ambiental's already consolidated identity was conducted to ensure coherence with the organization's visual ecosystem.

The research also involved defining a color palette and typographic families capable of balancing innovation and readability. During this process, the green tones already associated with Ambiental were preserved as an element of institutional continuity, while purple was incorporated as a new visual layer, reinforcing the project's technological character and increasing the identity's contrast.

## First Proposal: Flow in Triangular Shapes
The first version of the visual identity explored a construction based on connected triangular elements. The proposal sought to represent the stages of information circulation between user, server, and content, emphasizing the idea of flow and direction.

In addition to the logo, graphics, visual applications, and diagrams were developed to illustrate the path of information within the system. Although the solution translated part of the proposed concepts, the approach was not approved as it did not yet sufficiently represent the desired attributes for the project.

## Conceptual Refinement and New Approach
Based on the lessons learned in the first phase, a second round of development began. The concept was reformulated to approach a more technological, fluid, and contemporary language.

In this stage, new graphic assets were created, different typographic compositions were explored, and alternatives for symbols and graphics were developed. The references began to emphasize circuits, connections, digital interfaces, and data processing systems, replacing the more rigid logic of the previous proposal.

## The Approved Identity
The approved final version was built upon the idea that the visual identity should represent the very functioning of JOR-MCP: the path traveled by information from the user's request to the return of an organized response.

The visual direction began to rely on three main axes:
1. **Flow:** represented by connections, circuits, and graphic continuity;
2. **Processing:** expressed by circular shapes, intersections, and gradients;
3. **Clarity:** guaranteed by color contrast and element readability.

The logo was developed to communicate movement and connection, while circular graphics and gradients reinforce the idea of continuous data processing. The typography was updated to a more contemporary and technological language, maintaining high readability across different applications.

The result is a flexible visual identity capable of adapting to different contexts without losing consistency, visually translating the principles that guide JOR-MCP: information organization, reliability, and communication clarity.
